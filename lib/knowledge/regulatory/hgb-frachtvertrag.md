## Scope

The German Commercial Code (**Handelsgesetzbuch, HGB**) — not the BGB — governs commercial freight contracts. §§ 407 ff. HGB apply whenever goods are carried by land, inland waterway, or air as part of a commercial business (a "Frachtvertrag" between a sender/Absender and a carrier/Frachtführer). This is the domestic-law counterpart to the international CMR Convention: CMR applies to cross-border road carriage, HGB Frachtrecht applies to purely domestic German transport (and fills gaps CMR doesn't cover).

## Basic obligations (§ 407 HGB)

A Frachtvertrag obliges the carrier (Frachtführer) to transport the goods to their destination and deliver them to the consignee; the sender is obliged to pay the agreed freight charge. This is the general freight-contract framework underlying all commercial road transport in Germany.

## Special rules for household moves (§ 451 ff. HGB — Umzugsvertrag)

Moving contracts (Umzugsverträge) are treated as a distinct category within HGB freight law, with consumer-protective adjustments on top of the general Frachtvertrag rules — recognizing that a household move involves packing, disassembly/reassembly, and handling of personal belongings, not just point-to-point freight. § 451e HGB specifically sets the liability cap of EUR 620 per cubic metre for lost or damaged household goods (mirrored in the AMÖ standard terms — see that source), unless the parties agree otherwise.

## Practical relevance

For a German firm's day-to-day operations, HGB Frachtrecht/Umzugsrecht is the default legal backdrop for any domestic move or freight job (no border crossed). Once a job crosses an international border by road, CMR Convention rules take over instead of HGB. A firm's own quoted terms and AMÖ membership terms typically sit on top of this statutory floor — they can offer customers more, but not less, protection than what HGB guarantees.
