## What ADR covers

ADR ("Accord européen relatif au transport international des marchandises Dangereuses par Route") is the international agreement governing road transport of dangerous/hazardous goods. In Germany it is implemented via the Dangerous Goods Transport Act (**GGBefG**) and the Dangerous Goods Ordinance for road, rail and inland waterways (**GGVSEB**), which largely mirror the international ADR requirements for domestic transport too.

## The nine hazard classes

ADR divides all dangerous substances into nine classes (with several subclasses):

1. Explosives
2. Gases (2.1 flammable, 2.2 non-flammable/non-toxic, 2.3 toxic)
3. Flammable liquids (flash point ≤ 60 °C)
4. Flammable solids / substances liable to spontaneous combustion / substances that emit flammable gas on contact with water
5. Oxidizing substances and organic peroxides
6. Toxic and infectious substances
7. Radioactive material
8. Corrosive substances
9. Miscellaneous dangerous substances (e.g. lithium batteries, environmentally hazardous substances)

Each substance is identified by a **UN number** (a unique 4-digit international identifier), a **classification code**, and a **packaging group** (I = high danger, II = medium, III = low), which together determine labeling, packaging instructions, and documentation.

## Relevance to a moving/logistics company

Household and office moves rarely require an ADR-compliant transport in the strict sense, but crews still commonly encounter items that fall under ADR class boundaries and need special handling or refusal:
- lithium batteries in bulk or damaged devices (Class 9)
- compressed gas cylinders / camping gas bottles (Class 2)
- paints, solvents, cleaning chemicals above small quantity thresholds (Class 3/8)
- fireworks or ammunition (Class 1)

Where quantities stay below the ADR "limited quantity" / "excepted quantity" thresholds, standard transport is generally permitted without full ADR documentation — but this must be assessed case by case, not assumed. When in doubt, a firm should flag the item to a dangerous-goods-qualified colleague rather than load it on a standard move.

## Practical requirements when ADR does apply

Vehicles carrying goods above the relevant thresholds need ADR-compliant labeling/placarding, a transport document listing UN number and class, and in many cases a trained/certified driver and an appointed dangerous goods safety adviser (Gefahrgutbeauftragter) at the company.
